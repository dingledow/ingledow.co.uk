A prototype that makes it more playful to use revolving doors. Increasing the use of the revolving doors in the lobby of Northumbria University's School of Design building will improve its energy efficiency.

As part of being selected to enter Microsoft's Design Expo 2013, I worked within a small multi-disciplinary group in a workshop ran by Microsoft. Within five hours, we had to present a design problem and solution in the form of a physical prototype using .NET Gadgeteer.

We noticed that the energy efficiency of Northumbria University's School of Design was not great, especially considering how recently it was built. We noticed that most people entering the building would use the automated swing doors. These doors would stay open far longer than it would take for people to walk through, which is because they are designed for wheelchair access. The cold air entering the building as a result, decreases the effectiveness of the heating system in the lobby.

We decided to design a solution that would increase the usage of the revolving doors, which let a lot loss less

cold air in. The aim of this was to reduce the energy usage of the lobby.

Joyful Door makes it fun and playful to walk through the revolving doors. Using a gyroscope and an LCD screen connected together with a FEZ Spider .NET Gadgeteer kit, we used Visual Studio and C# to program the physical prototype.

When a user walks through the door, the image on the LCD screen changes from a sleeping face to a smiling face.

This project highlighted how everyday objects could respond to the way in which we use them to influence behaviour change.
